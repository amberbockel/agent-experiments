What just happened?

When the answer is obvious ("ice ___"), almost everyone agrees — including AI. When the answer is open ("a purple ___"), everyone disagrees and so does AI. That's the whole trick. AI predicts the most likely next word from patterns. When patterns are clear, it's reliable. When they aren't, it guesses.

The 4 words · 5 min

Four jargon words. Four real meanings.

Tap each card to flip it. These four words unlock 90% of how AI works.

Token
A puzzle piece of words
AI doesn't see whole words. It chops them into pieces (sometimes whole words, sometimes parts). "Unbelievable" might be 3 puzzle pieces: "un" + "believ" + "able".
Training
Reading the entire internet
An AI is "trained" by reading billions of webpages, books, and conversations. Not memorizing — finding patterns. Like reading 1,000 mystery novels until you can guess the killer.
Parameters
Tiny dials that got tuned
Picture billions of tiny dials inside the AI. Training adjusts each dial a little until the whole thing predicts good text. The biggest models have hundreds of billions of dials.
Hallucination
Confidently making stuff up
When AI doesn't know, it doesn't say "I don't know." It writes a confident-sounding answer that might be totally wrong. The trickiest thing about AI is that it sounds the same when it's right and when it's wrong.

The big idea

An AI is a probability machine. It calculates: "Given everything I've read, what word is most likely to come next?" That's it. There's no thinking, no understanding — just very, very good guessing.

Class question

If "mat" wins almost half the time, why doesn't AI always say "mat"? Why does it sometimes pick "floor" instead?

Because boring is bad.

If AI always picked the #1 word, every answer would feel robotic and identical. So AI rolls a weighted dice — usually the most likely word, but sometimes #2 or #3 to keep things interesting. That's why the same question can give you different answers each time.

The takeaway · 5 min

So... when should you use AI?

✓ AI is great for
  • Drafting — emails, essays, ideas
  • Brainstorming — when you're stuck
  • Explaining — concepts you don't get
  • Translating — languages, tone, audience
  • Coding — small programs, snippets
✗ AI is bad at
  • Today's news — it doesn't know
  • Math — it guesses, doesn't calculate
  • Specifics about you — your school, your friends
  • Telling truth from lies — sounds the same
  • Replacing your thinking — that's still your job

The three rules of using AI

  1. Treat it like a confident friend who's read a lot but sometimes makes stuff up.
  2. Always double-check anything that matters — facts, math, names, dates.
  3. Use it to start, not to finish. AI is great for the first draft. The good draft is still your job.
